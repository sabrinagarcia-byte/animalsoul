African penguin, on the verge of extinction?

The African penguin has been officially upgraded from “Endangered” to “Critically Endangered” on the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Red List, highlighting the species' grave risk of extinction. With a staggering 97% of its population already lost, the bird could disappear from the wild in less than 4,000 days unless urgent conservation efforts are made.


By Charlie Malcolm-McKay, in collaboration with BirdLife South Africa.


The southernmost tip of Africa is world-renowned for its incredible diversity and richness of wildlife. Every year, hundreds of thousands of visitors travel to South Africa and Namibia to experience the wonders of this nature. However, this beautiful coastal region also faces a devastating crisis: a man-made disaster that threatens the very existence of a unique species. The African penguin is now on the brink of extinction.


In the past, million of these birds once graced the coasts of South Africa and Namibia. Today, fewer than 32.000 remain in the wild - just 1% of the original population

With their distinctive tuxedo-like plumage and active social behavior, these small yet striking creatures are a common sight along the coast. African penguins are also essential for maintaining marine food chains and coastal ecosystems. However, their population has declined so drastically that they have now been downgraded from "Endangered" to "Critically Endangered" on the IUCN Red List.


One of the most pressing issues is the significant decline in food availability, driven primarily by competition with fishing and prey distribution. Intense fishing pressure in the waters surrounding their breeding colonies has led to a drastic reduction in small pelagic fish, essential to the penguins' diet. Warming sea temperatures and changes in ocean currents are altering the availability of their preferred prey, mainly sardines and anchovies, forcing young penguins to forage in increasingly unproductive areas. As a result, juvenile survival rates have plummeted, with reports of adult penguins abandoning their nests in a desperate search for food. While diseases such as avian influenza have also contributed to the population decline, studies highlight that the combined effects of commercial fishing and climate change remain the most critical threats to the African penguin population.
